background: >
  Genomics invited 3,000 MassMutual policyholders to free genetic testing. 54% created accounts
  from the landing page, but surveys revealed the remaining 46% lacked clarity on the product's
  value and worried about data privacy and hidden costs. The product needed a new identity and a
  redesigned site before the next cohort rollout. I led design from strategy through to launch,
  working with a small cross-functional team under a tight timeline.

problems_solutions:
  - problem: Users didn't understand what the product was or why it was free, causing distrust and drop-off before they reached the registration step.
    solution: Created a dedicated "How it works" section and FAQ explaining the MassMutual employer benefit, cost structure, and what the genetic testing process involved, in plain language.
  - problem: Privacy and data security concerns were a primary barrier. Users worried about what would happen to their genetic data.
    solution: Built a Trust & Privacy page addressing GDPR and ISO 27001 compliance, with plain-language explanations and clear answers to the questions raised in user research.
  - problem: The sample report in the hero didn't communicate how polygenic testing worked or why it was meaningful, but users found it confusing rather than reassuring.
    solution: Replaced the static screenshot with an animated visualisation showing how the test aggregates genetic data across diverse users, validated through two rounds of moderated usability testing.
  - problem: Users didn't know who was behind the product (no founders, no faces), which reduced trust in a health context where credibility matters.
    solution: Added an About Us page with founder and staff bios, moving 4 out of 5 usability test participants toward creating an account.
  - problem: Accuracy claims had no external frame of reference, making them feel like marketing rather than evidence.
    solution: Built a competitor accuracy comparison chart using published AUC data from 23andMe and Genomic Prediction, giving users a credible benchmark to evaluate the product against.

takeaways:
  - Sign-up rose from 54% to 59.3%, outperforming typical healthcare conversion benchmarks of below 40%.
  - Two rounds of moderated usability testing shaped every major decision. Intent to create an account rose from 2/5 to 4/5 after the first design iteration.
  - The Science page directly drove 3.2% of account creation, showing that trust-building content had measurable commercial value.
  - A new product identity ("Health Insights") was proposed by me, validated with target users, and launched as the public-facing brand.
